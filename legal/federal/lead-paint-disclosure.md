# Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (Title X, Section 1018)

## Quick Reference

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Full Name** | Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X), Section 1018 -- Disclosure Requirements |
| **Citation** | 42 U.S.C. S 4852d |
| **Enacted** | October 28, 1992 (effective September 6, 1996) |
| **Implementing Regulations** | 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart A (HUD); 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F (EPA) |
| **Enforcing Agencies** | EPA, HUD (joint enforcement) |

---

## Summary

Section 1018 of Title X requires sellers and lessors of residential housing built before 1978 to disclose known lead-based paint and lead-based paint hazards to prospective buyers and tenants. The law mandates specific disclosure forms, delivery of an EPA informational pamphlet, and provides buyers with a 10-day inspection period. Real estate agents have independent liability for ensuring compliance.

---

## Scope of Coverage

### Target Housing (Covered)
- All residential dwellings constructed before 1978
- Includes: single-family homes, apartments, condominiums, co-ops, lofts
- Includes: housing for the elderly (unless certified lead-free)
- Includes: government-assisted housing

### Exemptions
- Housing built in 1978 or later
- Zero-bedroom units (studios, dormitories, military barracks)
- Housing for the elderly or disabled (unless child under 6 resides or is expected to reside)
- Short-term vacation rentals (100 days or fewer)
- Housing that has been certified as lead-free by a certified inspector
- Foreclosure sales

---

## Requirements

### For Sellers (Sales Transactions)
1. **Disclose** known lead-based paint and/or lead-based paint hazards
2. **Provide** any available lead hazard evaluation reports (inspections, risk assessments, abatement records)
3. **Deliver** EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home"
4. **Allow** buyer a 10-day period to conduct lead inspection or risk assessment (parties may agree to a different time period or buyer may waive)
5. **Include** specific Lead Warning Statement in the contract
6. **Sign** the disclosure form (seller, buyer, and agents must all sign)
7. **Retain** signed disclosure and acknowledgment for 3 years from completion of sale

### For Lessors (Rental Transactions)
1. **Disclose** known lead-based paint and/or lead-based paint hazards
2. **Provide** any available lead hazard evaluation reports
3. **Deliver** EPA pamphlet
4. **Include** specific Lead Warning Statement in the lease
5. **Sign** the disclosure form
6. **Retain** signed disclosure for 3 years from commencement of leasing period

### For Real Estate Agents
1. **Ensure** seller/lessor is aware of and complies with disclosure obligations
2. **Ensure** required forms are completed and signed
3. **Retain** copies of disclosures for 3 years
4. **Independent liability** -- agent can be held liable even if seller fails to comply

### Contract Language Requirements

The following Lead Warning Statement must be included in every contract for sale of target housing:

> Every purchaser of any interest in residential real property on which a residential dwelling was built prior to 1978 is notified that such property may present exposure to lead from lead-based paint that may place young children at risk of developing lead poisoning. Lead poisoning in young children may produce permanent neurological damage, including learning disabilities, reduced intelligence quotient, behavioral problems, and impaired memory. Lead poisoning also poses a particular risk to pregnant women. The seller of any interest in residential real property is required to provide the buyer with any information on lead-based paint hazards from risk assessments or inspections in the seller's possession and notify the buyer of any known lead-based paint hazards. A risk assessment or inspection for possible lead-based paint hazards is recommended prior to purchase.

## Disclosure Form Content

The disclosure form must include:

### Seller's Disclosure (check one of each)
**Presence of Lead-Based Paint:**
- Known lead-based paint present (with explanation)
- No knowledge of lead-based paint

**Records and Reports:**
- Records/reports available (with list provided to buyer)
- No records or reports available

### Buyer's Acknowledgment (check one)
**Inspection Period:**
- Buyer received 10-day opportunity to conduct inspection
- Buyer waived the inspection opportunity

### Agent Acknowledgment
- Agent has informed seller of obligations
- Agent has ensured compliance with requirements

### Signatures
- Seller, buyer, and all agents must sign and date

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

### Civil Penalties
| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Per violation (TSCA Section 16) | Up to $19,507 (2025 adjusted) |
| Pattern of violations | Substantially higher cumulative penalties |
| Each day of continuing violation | May constitute separate violation |

### Private Lawsuits
- Treble damages (3x actual damages)
- Attorney fees and costs
- Joint and several liability for sellers and agents

### Criminal Penalties
- Knowing violations: criminal fines and potential imprisonment
- Rarely prosecuted but available for egregious cases

### Enforcement Actions
- EPA and HUD coordinate enforcement
- State and local agencies may have additional enforcement authority
- Targeted enforcement in high-risk areas

---

## Contract Implications

1. **Mandatory Contract Language:** Every purchase contract for pre-1978 housing must contain the Lead Warning Statement
2. **Inspection Contingency:** Buyer has a 10-day right to inspect (negotiable timeline, but cannot be eliminated unless buyer affirmatively waives)
3. **Contract Validity:** Failure to include lead paint disclosures may affect contract enforceability
4. **Agent Liability:** Real estate agents have independent duty to ensure compliance; listing and buyer agents both have obligations
5. **Closing Delays:** Lead inspection findings may trigger repair negotiations or contract termination
6. **Document Retention:** All parties must retain disclosure documents for 3 years

---

## Common Compliance Errors

1. Failing to provide the EPA pamphlet
2. Using outdated versions of the EPA pamphlet
3. Not including the Lead Warning Statement in the contract
4. Not providing the 10-day inspection opportunity (or documenting the waiver)
5. Agents not retaining copies of the signed disclosure
6. Not disclosing known lead hazards that seller was aware of
7. Misidentifying the year the property was built to avoid disclosure requirements
8. Failing to disclose lead-related repairs or abatement history
