## 1. Fair Housing Act (FHA)

- **Full Citation:** 42 U.S.C. SS 3601-3619
- **Enacted:** 1968 (Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968)
- **Major Amendments:** Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 (added familial status and disability as protected classes)
- **Implementing Regulation:** 24 CFR Part 100
- **Enforcing Agency:** HUD, DOJ
- **Detailed File:** [fair-housing-act.md](fair-housing-act.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

Prohibits discrimination in the sale, rental, advertising, or financing of housing based on seven protected classes:
- Race
- Color
- National origin
- Religion
- Sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity per 2021 HUD guidance)
- Familial status (families with children under 18)
- Disability (physical or mental)

### Who It Applies To

Agents, brokers, sellers, landlords, lenders, insurance companies, HOAs, MLS organizations, advertising platforms, and appraisers.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- All listing data must be free of discriminatory language
- Search filters cannot enable discriminatory filtering (e.g., filtering by race of neighborhood)
- Advertising and listing descriptions must comply with fair housing language guidelines
- Equal access to MLS membership and services regardless of protected class
- Platform cannot steer buyers toward or away from neighborhoods based on protected classes
- Photo and virtual tour content must not convey discriminatory preferences

### Penalties

- First offense: Up to $26,262 (2025 adjusted)
- Second offense (within 5 years): Up to $65,653
- Third or subsequent (within 7 years): Up to $131,308
- Unlimited compensatory and punitive damages in federal court
- Attorney fees and costs

### Contract Impact

- All real estate contracts must be offered on equal terms regardless of protected class
- Contract terms cannot vary based on buyer/seller membership in a protected class
- Fair housing disclosures and equal opportunity statements are standard in contracts

## 2. Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA)

- **Full Citation:** 12 U.S.C. SS 2601-2617
- **Enacted:** 1974
- **Major Amendments:** 1990 (escrow reform), 2010 (Dodd-Frank transferred authority to CFPB), 2015 (TRID integration)
- **Implementing Regulation:** Regulation X (12 CFR Part 1024)
- **Enforcing Agency:** CFPB
- **Detailed File:** [respa.md](respa.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Requires disclosure of settlement costs to borrowers
- Prohibits kickbacks and referral fees for settlement services (Section 8)
- Prohibits requiring excessive escrow deposits (Section 10)
- Requires Loan Estimate within 3 business days of application
- Requires Closing Disclosure at least 3 business days before closing
- Prohibits seller-required title insurance companies
- Requires affiliated business arrangement (AfBA) disclosures

### Who It Applies To

Lenders, mortgage brokers, loan servicers, settlement/closing agents, title companies, real estate agents, and brokers involved in federally related mortgage loans.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- No referral fee arrangements between MLS platform and settlement service providers
- Affiliated business disclosures if OneMLS partners with title, lending, or settlement services
- Settlement cost transparency tools must comply with RESPA requirements
- Marketing arrangements with lenders/title companies must avoid Section 8 violations

### Penalties

- Criminal: Up to $10,000 fine and/or 1 year imprisonment per violation (Section 8)
- Civil: Liability for up to 3x the settlement service charge
- CFPB enforcement actions with civil money penalties
- Pattern/practice violations: DOJ referral

### Contract Impact

- Settlement cost estimates must accompany mortgage applications
- Contracts cannot require use of specific settlement service providers as condition of sale
- Closing Disclosure must match contract terms

## 3. Truth in Lending Act (TILA)

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 1601-1666j
- **Enacted:** 1968 (Consumer Credit Protection Act, Title I)
- **Major Amendments:** 1980 (Simplification Act), 1994 (HOEPA), 2010 (Dodd-Frank), 2013 (TRID)
- **Implementing Regulation:** Regulation Z (12 CFR Part 1026)
- **Enforcing Agency:** CFPB
- **Detailed File:** [tila.md](tila.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Requires uniform disclosure of credit terms (APR, finance charges, payment schedule, total of payments)
- Provides right of rescission for certain mortgage transactions (3 days; extended to 3 years if disclosures not provided)
- Prohibits unfair lending practices for high-cost mortgages (HOEPA)
- Requires Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure (TRID integration with RESPA)
- Regulates advertising of credit terms
- Requires ability-to-repay determination for residential mortgages

### Who It Applies To

Creditors, mortgage lenders, mortgage brokers, advertisers of credit products.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Mortgage calculator tools must display APR correctly
- Any advertising of financing terms must comply with Regulation Z trigger terms
- If OneMLS displays loan estimates or payment calculators, disclosures must be accurate
- Platform cannot advertise misleading interest rates or loan terms

### Penalties

- Statutory damages: $200-$4,000 per individual violation; up to $1,000,000 for class actions
- Actual damages plus attorney fees
- Right of rescission (can void the loan entirely)
- CFPB civil money penalties
- Criminal penalties for willful violations: up to $5,000 fine and/or 1 year imprisonment

### Contract Impact

- Mortgage contracts must include standardized TILA disclosures
- Right of rescission applies to refinances and home equity loans (not purchase money mortgages)
- TRID requires integrated Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure for most closed-end residential mortgages

## 4. Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (Title X, Section 1018)

- **Full Citation:** 42 U.S.C. S 4852d
- **Enacted:** 1992 (Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act, Title X)
- **Implementing Regulation:** 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart A; 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F
- **Enforcing Agencies:** EPA, HUD
- **Detailed File:** [lead-paint-disclosure.md](lead-paint-disclosure.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Sellers and lessors of pre-1978 housing must disclose known lead-based paint and hazards
- Must provide EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home"
- Must allow buyers a 10-day period for lead inspection (can be negotiated)
- Contracts must include a specific Lead Warning Statement
- Sellers, lessors, and agents must retain disclosure records for 3 years
- Applies to all target housing (residential property built before 1978)

### Who It Applies To

Sellers, lessors, real estate agents, and property managers of pre-1978 residential housing.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Listings for pre-1978 properties should flag lead paint disclosure requirements
- Transaction workflows should include lead disclosure forms
- Year-built data should trigger lead paint compliance alerts
- Platform should provide access to EPA lead paint pamphlet
- Listing data should include lead paint disclosure status

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to $19,507 per violation (2025 adjusted) under TSCA Section 16
- Treble damages in private lawsuits
- Criminal penalties for knowing violations
- HUD and EPA joint enforcement

### Contract Impact

- Every contract for sale of pre-1978 housing must contain the Lead Warning Statement
- Buyer must acknowledge receipt of pamphlet and any known lead hazard reports
- 10-day inspection contingency (or waiver) must be documented in contract
- Agent liability for failure to ensure compliance

## 5. Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA)

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 1691-1691f
- **Enacted:** 1974
- **Major Amendments:** 1976 (expanded protected classes), 2010 (Dodd-Frank added appraisal copy requirement)
- **Implementing Regulation:** Regulation B (12 CFR Part 1002)
- **Enforcing Agencies:** CFPB, FTC, DOJ
- **Detailed File:** [ecoa.md](ecoa.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

Prohibits discrimination in any aspect of a credit transaction based on:
- Race, color, religion, national origin
- Sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity)
- Marital status
- Age (if applicant has capacity to contract)
- Receipt of public assistance income
- Good-faith exercise of rights under the Consumer Credit Protection Act

Additional requirements:
- Creditors must provide adverse action notices with specific reasons for denial
- Creditors must provide copies of appraisals for first-lien dwelling-secured loans
- Income from public assistance, alimony, or child support cannot be discounted

### Who It Applies To

Any person who regularly participates in credit decisions: banks, credit unions, mortgage companies, finance companies, retailers.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- If OneMLS partners with or refers to lenders, those lenders must comply with ECOA
- Platform should not collect or display information that could facilitate credit discrimination
- Mortgage pre-qualification tools must not discriminate based on protected classes
- Any data analytics or algorithms used in lending referrals must be bias-free

### Penalties

- Actual damages (no cap)
- Punitive damages up to $10,000 (individual) or lesser of $500,000 or 1% of net worth (class action)
- Attorney fees and costs
- Injunctive and declaratory relief
- CFPB and DOJ enforcement actions

### Contract Impact

- Credit terms in real estate transactions cannot vary based on protected characteristics
- Adverse action notices required when credit applications are denied
- Appraisal copies must be provided to applicants for first-lien loans

## 6. Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act

- **Full Citation:** 12 U.S.C. SS 5301-5641; Pub. L. 111-203
- **Enacted:** 2010
- **Key Title for Real Estate:** Title XIV (Mortgage Reform and Anti-Predatory Lending Act)
- **Implementing Regulations:** Various CFPB rules under Regulations X and Z
- **Enforcing Agency:** CFPB
- **Detailed File:** [dodd-frank.md](dodd-frank.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

**Title XIV -- Mortgage Reform:**
- Ability-to-Repay (ATR) requirement: Lenders must verify borrower can repay
- Qualified Mortgage (QM) standards: Maximum 43% DTI, restrictions on fees and points
- Prohibits steering borrowers to higher-cost loans when they qualify for lower-cost options
- Restricts balloon payments, negative amortization, and prepayment penalties
- Requires higher appraisal standards for higher-risk mortgages
- Created TRID (TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure) -- "Know Before You Owe"
- Established mortgage loan originator (MLO) licensing and registration requirements
- Restricts seller financing (exemptions for owners selling 1-3 properties per year)

**Other Titles:**
- Created the CFPB as the primary federal consumer financial protection agency
- Enhanced oversight of systemically important financial institutions
- Volcker Rule (limits bank proprietary trading)

### Who It Applies To

Mortgage lenders, loan originators, banks, servicers, appraisers, seller-financers (with exemptions).

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Any mortgage tools or calculators must reflect current QM standards
- MLO licensing verification if platform connects buyers with loan originators
- TRID timeline awareness in transaction management tools
- Seller financing listings must note Dodd-Frank restrictions
- Appraisal independence requirements if platform facilitates appraisal ordering

### Penalties

- CFPB enforcement: Civil money penalties tiered by violation severity
  - Tier 1 (any violation): Up to $6,703 per day
  - Tier 2 (reckless): Up to $33,517 per day
  - Tier 3 (knowing): Up to $1,340,678 per day
- Rescission rights for borrowers
- Criminal referrals for fraud
- State AG enforcement authority

### Contract Impact

- Mortgage contracts must meet QM standards to receive safe harbor/rebuttable presumption
- TRID disclosures (Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure) are integrated into the contract process
- Seller-financed transactions have restrictions on loan terms
- ATR documentation requirements are part of the loan file

## 7. Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

- **Full Citation:** 42 U.S.C. SS 12101-12213
- **Enacted:** 1990
- **Major Amendments:** ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA)
- **Implementing Regulations:** 28 CFR Parts 35-36
- **Enforcing Agency:** DOJ
- **Detailed File:** [ada.md](ada.md)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- **Title II:** State and local government facilities must be accessible
- **Title III:** Public accommodations and commercial facilities must be accessible
  - Includes real estate offices, brokerage firms, model homes, leasing offices
  - New construction must be accessible; existing facilities must remove barriers where readily achievable
  - Reasonable modifications to policies, practices, and procedures
- **Website Accessibility:** DOJ has issued guidance that ADA applies to websites of public accommodations

### Who It Applies To

Real estate brokerages, property management companies, commercial property owners, MLS organizations (as providers of services to the public), government housing agencies.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Platform must be WCAG 2.1 AA compliant (web accessibility)
- Screen reader compatibility for all listing data
- Alternative text for all property images
- Accessible search and filter interfaces
- Keyboard navigation support throughout the platform
- Accessible document formats for contracts and disclosures
- Video content should include captions
- Color contrast ratios must meet accessibility standards

### Penalties

- Civil penalties: Up to $75,000 for first violation, $150,000 for subsequent violations
- DOJ enforcement actions
- Private lawsuits for injunctive relief and attorney fees
- State and local accessibility laws may impose additional penalties
- No cap on compensatory damages in some jurisdictions

### Contract Impact

- Contracts for commercial real estate must address ADA compliance responsibilities
- Leases should allocate responsibility for ADA modifications between landlord and tenant
- Property condition disclosures should note ADA compliance status
- Reasonable accommodation requests must be considered in housing transactions

## 8. Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA)

- **Full Citation:** 12 U.S.C. SS 2801-2810
- **Enacted:** 1975
- **Major Amendments:** 2010 (Dodd-Frank expanded data collection), 2015 (CFPB final rule)
- **Implementing Regulation:** Regulation C (12 CFR Part 1003)
- **Enforcing Agency:** CFPB, FFIEC

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Requires covered financial institutions to collect, report, and disclose mortgage lending data
- Data includes: loan applications, originations, purchases, denials, and other dispositions
- Expanded data points include: property value, loan term, rate spread, credit score, DTI, combined LTV, age, and more
- Public disclosure of lending data to identify discriminatory patterns
- Annual Loan/Application Register (LAR) filing

### Who It Applies To

Banks, savings associations, credit unions, and for-profit non-depository mortgage lending institutions meeting coverage thresholds.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- If OneMLS aggregates or displays lending data, it must be sourced from HMDA-reported data
- Market analytics tools should leverage HMDA data for fair lending analysis
- Platform should not facilitate lending patterns that would create HMDA compliance issues
- Data privacy for individual borrower information

### Penalties

- Civil money penalties assessed by prudential regulators
- Enforcement actions by CFPB
- Public disclosure of non-compliance
- Reputational risk from disclosed lending patterns

### Contract Impact

- Does not directly affect contract terms
- Lending data collection occurs during the mortgage origination process
- Lenders must collect demographic data from applicants (voluntary for applicants)

## 9. Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 1681-1681x
- **Enacted:** 1970
- **Major Amendments:** 2003 (FACTA), 2010 (Dodd-Frank)
- **Implementing Regulation:** Regulation V (12 CFR Part 1022)
- **Enforcing Agencies:** CFPB, FTC

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Regulates collection, dissemination, and use of consumer credit information
- Requires permissible purpose to access credit reports
- Requires consumer notification when adverse action is taken based on credit report
- Requires credit score disclosure for loans secured by residential real property
- Mandates dispute resolution procedures
- Limits reporting of negative information (generally 7 years; bankruptcies 10 years)

### Who It Applies To

Consumer reporting agencies (CRAs), furnishers of information (creditors), users of consumer reports (landlords, lenders, employers).

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- If OneMLS accesses or integrates credit data, it must have permissible purpose
- Tenant screening features must comply with FCRA adverse action requirements
- Any credit-based pre-qualification tools must follow FCRA guidelines
- Data security requirements for any consumer credit information stored or transmitted

### Penalties

- Statutory damages: $100-$1,000 per willful violation
- Actual damages for negligent violations
- Punitive damages for willful non-compliance
- Class action liability
- Attorney fees and costs
- Criminal penalties: Up to $2,500 fine and/or up to 2 years imprisonment

### Contract Impact

- Credit report usage must be authorized by the consumer
- Adverse action notices required when denying housing based on credit reports
- Credit score disclosures required at or before consummation for residential loans

## 10. Fair and Accurate Credit Transactions Act (FACTA)

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 1681 et seq. (amending FCRA); Pub. L. 108-159
- **Enacted:** 2003
- **Implementing Regulation:** Identity Theft Red Flags Rule (16 CFR Part 681)
- **Enforcing Agencies:** FTC, CFPB

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Identity Theft Red Flags Rule: Covered entities must implement written identity theft prevention programs
- Disposal Rule: Proper disposal of consumer report information
- Truncation of credit card numbers on receipts
- Free annual credit reports for consumers
- Fraud alerts and active duty alerts on credit reports
- Address discrepancy procedures for consumer reporting agencies

### Who It Applies To

Financial institutions, creditors (including those who regularly arrange credit, potentially including real estate agents), consumer reporting agencies.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- If the platform handles credit data, must implement Red Flags identity theft prevention program
- Proper disposal of any consumer report data
- Secure handling of financial information in transaction management
- Address verification procedures for user accounts

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to $3,500 per violation (FTC enforcement)
- State AG enforcement
- Private right of action under FCRA provisions

### Contract Impact

- Limited direct impact on real estate contracts
- Identity verification procedures may affect transaction timelines
- Proper disposal of application and credit documents post-closing

## 11. CAN-SPAM Act

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 7701-7713
- **Enacted:** 2003
- **Implementing Regulation:** 16 CFR Part 316 (FTC Rule)
- **Enforcing Agency:** FTC

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Prohibits false or misleading header information in commercial emails
- Prohibits deceptive subject lines
- Requires identification of message as advertisement
- Requires valid physical postal address in commercial emails
- Requires clear opt-out mechanism
- Opt-out requests must be honored within 10 business days
- Prohibits selling or transferring email addresses of opt-out requesters
- Prohibits harvesting email addresses from websites

### Who It Applies To

Any person or entity sending commercial email messages, including real estate agents, brokers, MLS platforms, lenders, and property managers.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- All platform-generated marketing emails must include opt-out mechanism
- Property alert and notification emails must comply with CAN-SPAM if commercial in nature
- Physical address must be included in all commercial emails
- Email list management must respect opt-out requests within 10 business days
- Agent/broker marketing tools on the platform must enforce CAN-SPAM compliance
- Transactional emails (account notifications, listing updates) have different requirements

### Penalties

- Up to $53,088 per email violation (2025 adjusted)
- Aggravated violations can lead to criminal charges
- No private right of action (FTC and state AG enforcement only)

### Contract Impact

- No direct impact on real estate contracts
- Affects marketing and communication practices around transactions

## 12. Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA)

- **Full Citation:** 47 U.S.C. S 227
- **Enacted:** 1991
- **Major Amendments:** 2012 (FCC rules requiring prior express written consent for autodialed/prerecorded calls), 2015 (FCC Declaratory Ruling)
- **Implementing Rules:** 47 CFR Part 64
- **Enforcing Agency:** FCC

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Requires prior express written consent for autodialed or prerecorded telemarketing calls/texts
- Establishes Do Not Call Registry compliance requirements
- Restricts robocalls and autodialed calls to cell phones
- Requires identification of caller at beginning of message
- Restricts calling hours (before 8 AM or after 9 PM local time)
- Requires maintenance of internal do-not-call lists
- Prohibits unsolicited fax advertisements

### Who It Applies To

Real estate agents, brokers, brokerages, mortgage companies, property managers, and any entity making telemarketing calls or texts.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Contact tools must integrate Do Not Call Registry checking
- Lead distribution systems must verify TCPA consent
- SMS/text notification features must obtain prior express written consent
- Agent communication tools must track consent status
- Autodialer features (if any) must comply with TCPA requirements
- Maintain records of consent for all contacts

### Penalties

- $500 per violation (per call/text)
- $1,500 per willful violation (treble damages)
- No cap on total damages (strict liability)
- Class action exposure (average settlements $5-15 million)
- FCC enforcement actions and fines

### Contract Impact

- No direct impact on real estate contract terms
- Marketing practices around transactions must comply
- Consent documentation should be maintained as part of client records

## 13. FIRREA Title XI (Appraisal Requirements)

- **Full Citation:** 12 U.S.C. SS 3331-3351
- **Enacted:** 1989 (Financial Institutions Reform, Recovery, and Enforcement Act)
- **Major Amendments:** 2018 (EGRRCPA raised thresholds)
- **Implementing Regulations:** Various (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve appraisal regulations)
- **Enforcing Agencies:** Federal banking regulators, Appraisal Subcommittee (ASC)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Real estate appraisals for federally related transactions must be performed by state-licensed or certified appraisers
- Appraisals must conform to Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP)
- Appraisals must be in writing
- Residential transactions over $400,000 require state-certified appraisers
- Commercial transactions over $500,000 require state-certified appraisers
- Below-threshold transactions still require evaluations
- Appraisal independence requirements

### Who It Applies To

Appraisers, lenders, and any party to a federally related real estate transaction.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- If platform includes appraisal ordering or management, must comply with independence requirements
- Appraiser credentials should be verifiable through ASC National Registry
- AVM (Automated Valuation Model) tools must comply with federal guidelines
- Property valuation displays should include appropriate disclaimers

### Penalties

- Regulatory sanctions against lenders for using unqualified appraisers
- State licensing board actions against appraisers
- Loan-level consequences (loan may be deemed non-conforming)
- Civil liability for negligent appraisals

### Contract Impact

- Appraisal contingencies are standard in purchase contracts for financed transactions
- Contract timelines must accommodate appraisal ordering and completion
- Appraisal shortfalls affect contract negotiations

## 14. Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA)

- **Full Citation:** 26 U.S.C. SS 897, 1445
- **Enacted:** 1980
- **Major Amendments:** 2016 (PATH Act increased withholding to 15%)
- **Enforcing Agency:** IRS

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Requires withholding of 15% of gross sales price when seller is a "foreign person"
- Foreign persons must pay U.S. income tax on gains from U.S. real property dispositions
- Buyers must file Form 8288 and 8288-A within 20 days of closing
- Reduced withholding (10%) for residences sold for $1 million or less when buyer intends personal use
- Exemption for residences sold for $300,000 or less when buyer intends personal use
- Seller may apply for withholding certificate to reduce or eliminate withholding

### Who It Applies To

Foreign sellers of U.S. real property interests, buyers of property from foreign sellers, settlement agents, title companies, attorneys.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Transaction management tools should flag potential FIRPTA transactions
- Seller nationality/residency status fields in listing data
- FIRPTA withholding calculator tools
- Non-foreign affidavit (FIRPTA certificate) templates
- Alert system for agents when foreign seller is identified

### Penalties

- Buyer liability for failing to withhold (liable for 15% of gross price plus interest and penalties)
- IRS penalties and interest for late filing
- Criminal penalties for willful failure to file

### Contract Impact

- Contracts with foreign sellers must address FIRPTA withholding
- FIRPTA affidavit (non-foreign certification) is standard part of closing documents
- Withholding affects net proceeds calculation at closing
- May require IRS withholding certificate application, adding time to closing

## 15. Interstate Land Sales Full Disclosure Act (ILSA)

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 1701-1720
- **Enacted:** 1968
- **Implementing Regulation:** Regulation J (12 CFR Part 1010)
- **Enforcing Agency:** CFPB (transferred from HUD)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Developers of subdivisions of 100+ lots must register with CFPB
- Must provide buyers with a Property Report before signing a contract
- Prohibits fraud and misrepresentation in land sales
- Provides buyers a right to revoke contract within 7 days of signing or receipt of Property Report
- Right to void contract within 2 years if Property Report not provided

### Who It Applies To

Land developers, subdividers, and their agents selling or leasing lots in subdivisions of 25 or more lots through interstate commerce.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Subdivision listings should verify ILSA registration status where applicable
- New development listings should include Property Report availability
- Platform should flag subdivision listings that may trigger ILSA requirements

### Penalties

- Civil fines and injunctive relief
- Private right of action for damages
- Buyer right to void contract plus interest, costs, and attorney fees
- Criminal penalties: Up to $10,000 fine and/or 5 years imprisonment

### Contract Impact

- Contracts for unregistered subdivisions may be voidable
- 7-day right of revocation must be preserved
- Property Report must be delivered before contract execution

## 16. Flood Disaster Protection Act (FDPA)

- **Full Citation:** 42 U.S.C. SS 4001-4129 (National Flood Insurance Act of 1968 and Flood Disaster Protection Act of 1973)
- **Major Amendments:** National Flood Insurance Reform Act of 1994, Biggert-Waters Act of 2012
- **Implementing Regulation:** 44 CFR Parts 59-77 (FEMA); banking regulators' flood rules
- **Enforcing Agencies:** FEMA, federal banking regulators

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Mandatory flood insurance for properties in Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs) with federally backed mortgages
- Lenders must determine flood zone status before closing
- Flood insurance must equal lesser of outstanding loan balance or maximum NFIP coverage
- Notice to borrowers regarding flood hazard status
- Force-placement of flood insurance if borrower lapses coverage
- Community participation in NFIP as prerequisite for federally backed loans

### Who It Applies To

Lenders, mortgage servicers, property owners in SFHAs, communities participating in NFIP.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Property listings should include flood zone designation from FEMA flood maps
- Flag properties in SFHAs for flood insurance requirements
- Integration with FEMA flood map data
- Flood zone data in property details and disclosures

### Penalties

- Lender penalties up to $2,000 per violation for failure to require flood insurance
- Pattern violations can result in civil money penalties
- Regulatory enforcement actions against lenders

### Contract Impact

- Flood insurance requirement affects financing contingencies
- Flood zone status disclosure is part of property condition reporting
- Cost of flood insurance can materially affect transaction economics

## 17. Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN)

- **Full Citation:** 15 U.S.C. SS 7001-7006
- **Enacted:** 2000
- **Related State Law:** Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA) -- adopted by most states
- **Enforcing Agency:** FTC (general enforcement)

### What It Requires/Prohibits

- Electronic signatures and records cannot be denied legal validity solely because they are electronic
- Consumer consent required before providing electronic disclosures
- Consumers must be informed of right to receive paper documents
- Certain exceptions: wills, family law documents, court orders, utility cancellations
- Limitations for foreclosure, eviction, acceleration, and default notices on primary residences
- Does not mandate acceptance of electronic signatures

### Who It Applies To

All parties to real estate transactions who use electronic signatures or records.

### Key Compliance for OneMLS

- Platform e-signature integration must comply with E-SIGN and applicable state UETA
- Consumer consent flows for electronic disclosures
- Record retention requirements for electronic documents
- Ability for consumers to opt for paper documents
- Audit trails for electronic signatures (who, when, what)
- Compliance with exceptions for certain real estate documents

### Penalties

- Contracts signed electronically may be challenged if E-SIGN requirements not met
- State-specific penalties under UETA
- No federal private right of action under E-SIGN itself

### Contract Impact

- Electronic signatures are valid for most real estate contracts
- Consumer consent must be obtained before electronic delivery of disclosures
- Some documents (foreclosure notices, eviction notices) may still require paper delivery
- State-specific requirements may add additional e-signature rules

## Additional Federal Laws with Peripheral Impact

### Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA)
- 15 U.S.C. SS 6801-6809
- Requires financial institutions to explain information-sharing practices and safeguard sensitive data
- Applies to real estate settlement companies, mortgage lenders, and potentially MLS platforms handling financial data

### Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) / Anti-Money Laundering (AML)
- 31 U.S.C. SS 5311-5332
- Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) and Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) for large cash transactions
- FinCEN Geographic Targeting Orders for all-cash real estate purchases in certain areas
- Proposed FinCEN rules to extend BSA/AML to real estate professionals

### PATRIOT Act (Title III)
- Pub. L. 107-56
- Enhanced due diligence for financial transactions
- Customer identification programs (CIP)
- Applies to settlement agents and financial institutions in real estate transactions

### IRS Reporting Requirements
- 26 U.S.C. S 6045(e) -- Information returns for real estate transactions
- Form 1099-S reporting for gross proceeds from real property sales
- Real estate professionals responsible for filing in many cases

### Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA)
- 50 U.S.C. SS 3901-4043
- Protects active-duty military members in real estate transactions
- Interest rate caps (6%) on pre-service mortgages
- Restrictions on foreclosure and eviction of servicemembers
- Lease termination rights for servicemembers
