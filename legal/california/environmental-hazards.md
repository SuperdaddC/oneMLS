# Environmental Hazard Disclosures

> **Primary Authority:** Various federal and state statutes (see below)
> **Related Forms:** C.A.R. FLD (Federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosures); TDS environmental sections
> **Booklet:** "Residential Environmental Hazards: A Guide for Homeowners, Buyers, and Renters" (CalEPA/CDPH)

---

## Overview

California requires disclosure of known environmental hazards on residential property. These requirements come from both federal law (lead-based paint) and state law (various Civil Code, Health and Safety Code, and Proposition 65 provisions). Sellers must disclose known environmental hazards, and agents must report observable conditions from their visual inspections.

---

## 1. Lead-Based Paint Disclosure

### Federal Law
- **Authority:** Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X); 42 USC 4852d; 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart F
- **Scope:** All residential properties built **before 1978**
- **C.A.R. Form:** FLD (Federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosures)

### Requirements

**Seller Must:**
1. Disclose any known lead-based paint and/or lead-based paint hazards in the property
2. Provide buyers with any available records or reports pertaining to lead-based paint
3. Provide the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home"
4. Allow buyer a 10-day period to conduct a lead inspection or risk assessment (can be modified by agreement)

**Agent Must:**
1. Ensure seller is made aware of disclosure obligations
2. Ensure seller provides required disclosures
3. Retain signed acknowledgments for 3 years

### Form Requirements
The FLD form must include:
- Seller's disclosure of known lead paint or hazards
- List of available reports or records
- Buyer's acknowledgment of receipt of information
- Agent certifications
- Signatures of all parties

### Penalties
- Civil: Up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation)
- Treble damages in private lawsuits
- Criminal: Knowing violations may result in criminal penalties
- Agent liability: Joint and several liability for failure to ensure compliance

### Exemptions
- Properties built in 1978 or later
- Zero-bedroom units (studios, lofts, dormitories)
- Housing for the elderly (unless children are expected to reside)
- Foreclosure sales (but disclosure still required to subsequent buyer)

## 2. Asbestos

### California Law
- **Authority:** California Health and Safety Code; CalOSHA regulations (8 CCR 1529)
- **TDS Requirement:** Seller must disclose known asbestos presence on the TDS

### Disclosure Requirements
- Sellers must disclose actual knowledge of asbestos-containing materials (ACM) in the property
- Common locations: insulation, floor tiles, ceiling tiles, roofing, siding, pipe wrap, popcorn ceilings (pre-1980)
- No mandatory testing requirement for residential sales
- If asbestos is known, disclosure of location and condition is required

### Agent's Role
- Visual inspection may reveal suspected ACM (textured ceilings, old pipe insulation)
- Agent should recommend professional inspection if ACM is suspected
- Agent should note observations on AVID form

### Remediation
- Intact asbestos that is undisturbed is generally not an immediate hazard
- Removal, encapsulation, or enclosure must be performed by licensed professionals
- California requires notification to local Air Quality Management District before removal

## 3. Mold

### California Law
- **Authority:** Health and Safety Code 26100-26156 (Toxic Mold Protection Act of 2001); Civil Code 1102 (TDS)
- **CDPH Guidelines:** California Department of Public Health has established that visible mold is a substandard condition

### Disclosure Requirements
- Seller must disclose known mold presence on TDS
- TDS asks if seller is aware of any hazardous substances
- If seller checks "Yes" to mold awareness, written explanation required detailing:
  - Location of mold
  - Extent of the issue
  - Remediation efforts undertaken

### Agent's Role
- Visual inspection should note visible mold, water stains, or musty odors
- Agent should recommend professional mold inspection if suspected
- Mold presence may indicate underlying water intrusion issues

### Key Points
- There is no specific California statute setting "permissible mold levels" in residential properties
- CDPH treats visible mold as a health hazard
- Seller cannot disclaim knowledge of visible mold

## 4. Radon Gas

### California Law
- **Authority:** Health and Safety Code 25249.5 et seq. (Proposition 65); Environmental Hazards booklet
- **TDS Requirement:** TDS asks about awareness of radon gas

### Disclosure Requirements
- Seller must disclose actual knowledge of elevated radon levels
- No mandatory testing required for residential sales in California
- The "Residential Environmental Hazards" booklet (provided to all buyers) includes information about radon

### Risk Areas
- Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas from uranium decay in soil
- Parts of California (Foothill areas, Ventura County, Santa Barbara County, portions of the Sierra Nevada) have higher radon potential
- EPA recommends testing in all homes below the third floor

---

## 5. Formaldehyde

### California Law
- **Authority:** Health and Safety Code; Civil Code 1102 (TDS)
- **TDS Requirement:** TDS asks about awareness of formaldehyde

### Disclosure Requirements
- Seller must disclose known presence of formaldehyde
- Common sources: urea-formaldehyde foam insulation (UFFI), certain pressed-wood products, carpeting

### Key Points
- California Proposition 65 lists formaldehyde as a known carcinogen
- UFFI was commonly used in homes through the early 1980s
- Modern building materials contain much lower formaldehyde levels

---

## 6. Underground Storage Tanks (USTs)

### California Law
- **Authority:** Health and Safety Code 25280-25299.8 (Underground Storage Tank Program); Civil Code 1102 (TDS)
- **TDS Requirement:** TDS asks about fuel or chemical storage tanks

### Disclosure Requirements
- Seller must disclose knowledge of current or former underground storage tanks
- Must disclose known or suspected soil or groundwater contamination
- Former gas station sites and agricultural properties are high-risk

### Regulatory Framework
- California State Water Resources Control Board oversees UST cleanup
- Property may be listed on environmental databases (GeoTracker, EnviroStor)
- Cleanup obligations may transfer with property ownership

---

## 7. Contaminated Soil and Water

### California Law
- **Authority:** Health and Safety Code 25300 et seq. (Hazardous Substance Account Act); Civil Code 1102 (TDS)

### Disclosure Requirements
- Seller must disclose knowledge of contaminated soil or groundwater
- Must disclose proximity to known contamination sites
- Environmental databases (DTSC EnviroStor, SWRCB GeoTracker) are publicly available

---

## 8. Proposition 65 (Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986)

### Authority
- Health and Safety Code 25249.5 - 25249.13

### Application to Real Estate
- Proposition 65 requires businesses with 10+ employees to provide warnings before exposing individuals to listed chemicals
- Applies to commercial properties and common areas in residential complexes
- Property managers and HOAs may need to post Prop 65 warnings
- Individual residential sales are generally not subject to Prop 65 warning requirements (seller is not typically a "business")
- However, the seller must still disclose known chemical hazards on the TDS

---

## 9. Methamphetamine Contamination

### California Law
- **Authority:** Health and Safety Code 25400.10 et seq.
- **Mandatory Decontamination:** Properties used for meth manufacturing must be assessed and decontaminated before sale or rental

### Disclosure Requirements
- Local health officer must be notified of known meth labs
- Property may be placed on a contaminated properties list
- Seller must disclose known history of meth production or contamination
- Decontamination must meet standards set by DTSC

---

## 10. Electromagnetic Fields (EMFs)

### California Position
- No specific statutory disclosure requirement for EMFs
- However, proximity to high-voltage power lines may be a material fact
- Agent should note visible power line proximity during visual inspection
- Some buyers consider this a material concern -- best practice is to disclose if asked

## Environmental Hazards Booklet

### Required Delivery
- The "Residential Environmental Hazards: A Guide for Homeowners, Buyers, and Renters" booklet must be provided to the buyer
- Published by the California Environmental Protection Agency and Department of Public Health
- Covers: lead, asbestos, radon, formaldehyde, mold, household hazardous waste
- Delivery satisfies certain disclosure requirements (serves as a general education tool)

---

## Penalties for Non-Disclosure

| Hazard | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Lead paint (federal) | Up to $19,507/violation; treble damages; criminal penalties |
| Known hazards on TDS | Actual damages (Civil Code 1102.13) |
| Willful concealment | Actual damages + potential punitive damages; fraud liability |
| Agent failure to disclose observations | DRE discipline; actual damages |
| Prop 65 violations | $2,500/day/violation (business context) |

## Contract Builder Implementation Notes

### Detection Logic
1. **Year built** -- trigger lead paint disclosure for pre-1978 properties
2. **Property address** -- cross-reference with environmental databases (GeoTracker, EnviroStor)
3. **Seller questionnaire** -- ask about each environmental hazard category
4. **Agent inspection** -- capture observations related to environmental conditions

### Required Documents by Scenario
- Pre-1978 home: FLD form + EPA pamphlet + Environmental Hazards booklet
- Known contamination: Environmental site assessment documentation
- All transactions: TDS environmental sections + Environmental Hazards booklet

### Validation Rules
- If year built < 1978: lead paint disclosure form is mandatory
- Environmental hazard responses on TDS must not be left blank
- If any hazard is disclosed, require written explanation
- Track delivery of Environmental Hazards booklet
