# Purchase Agreement Requirements

> **Primary Authority:** California Civil Code 1624 (Statute of Frauds); general contract law
> **Industry Standard Form:** C.A.R. Residential Purchase Agreement (RPA)
> **Related:** Civil Code 1549-1701 (Contracts generally)

---

## Overview

A real estate purchase agreement in California must satisfy the Statute of Frauds (Civil Code 1624) by being in writing and signed by the party to be charged. The California Association of REALTORS (C.A.R.) publishes the standard Residential Purchase Agreement (RPA) form used in most residential transactions, though any written agreement meeting legal requirements is valid.

---

## Legal Requirements for a Valid Purchase Agreement

### Statute of Frauds (Civil Code 1624)
All real estate purchase agreements must be:
1. **In writing** -- oral agreements for real property transfers are unenforceable
2. **Signed** by the party to be charged (or their authorized agent)
3. **Sufficiently definite** in material terms

See [statute-of-frauds.md](statute-of-frauds.md) for detailed analysis.

### Essential Terms
A valid purchase agreement must identify with reasonable certainty:
1. **Parties** -- full legal names of buyer(s) and seller(s)
2. **Property** -- address, legal description, or APN sufficient to identify the property
3. **Price** -- the purchase price or a formula for determining it
4. **Terms** -- how the purchase price will be paid (cash, financing terms)
5. **Signatures** -- signed by the party to be charged

### Additional Standard Terms
While not strictly required for contract formation, the following are standard in California residential transactions:
- Earnest money deposit amount and terms
- Financing contingency terms
- Inspection contingency terms
- Appraisal contingency terms
- Closing (escrow) date
- Possession date
- Personal property included/excluded
- Allocation of closing costs
- Dispute resolution provisions

## C.A.R. Residential Purchase Agreement (RPA)

### Structure and Key Sections

The C.A.R. RPA is the industry-standard form and includes the following major sections:

**1. Offer Terms**
- Purchase price
- Initial deposit amount, form, and delivery timeline
- Increased deposit (if applicable)
- Loan terms (type, amount, interest rate, term)
- Balance of down payment
- Closing and possession dates

**2. Finance Terms**
- Loan contingency (typically 21 days)
- Appraisal contingency
- All-cash offer provisions
- Verification of funds

**3. Allocation of Costs**
- Escrow fees (typically split or per local custom)
- Title insurance (owner's policy typically paid by seller in Southern CA; varies by region)
- County transfer tax (typically seller)
- City transfer tax (if applicable)
- HOA transfer fees
- Home warranty (who pays and coverage)
- Natural hazard disclosure report
- Inspections

**4. Statutory and Other Disclosures**
- TDS delivery timeline
- NHD delivery
- Lead-based paint disclosure
- Megan's Law database disclosure (built into form)
- Preliminary title report
- Condominium/HOA documents

**5. Contingencies**
- Inspection contingency (typically 17 days)
- Loan contingency (typically 21 days)
- Appraisal contingency
- Title review contingency
- Contingency removal process (active removal required in C.A.R. forms)

**6. Condition of Property**
- Property sold in present condition (or with agreed repairs)
- Seller's obligation to maintain property through close
- Seller's obligation to disclose changes in property condition

**7. Buyer's Investigation**
- Right to investigate all matters affecting the property
- Professional inspections
- Government compliance
- Permits and code violations

**8. Closing and Possession**
- Close of escrow date
- Possession delivery (typically at close)
- Keys, security systems, access
- Prorations (taxes, HOA dues, rent)

**9. Dispute Resolution**
- Mediation (mandatory before arbitration or litigation)
- Arbitration (optional -- requires separate initials by both parties)
- Attorney fees provision

**10. Agency Confirmation**
- Confirms agency relationships
- Identifies listing and selling agents and brokerages

**11. Buyer and Seller Compensation**
- Post-NAR settlement: explicit disclosure of how buyer's agent is compensated
- Seller may agree to pay buyer's agent compensation
- Buyer responsible for any shortfall between seller's offer and buyer-broker agreement amount

## Earnest Money Deposit

### Requirements
- No statutory minimum, but industry standard is 1-3% of purchase price
- Must be deposited within the timeframe specified in the agreement (C.A.R. RPA: 3 business days after acceptance)
- Must be deposited into escrow or broker trust account

### Deposit Disputes
- If the transaction fails, both parties must agree to release of deposit
- If parties cannot agree, funds remain in escrow
- Dispute resolution per the agreement's mediation/arbitration provisions
- Interpleader action available if escrow holder cannot resolve

## Contingencies

### Inspection Contingency
- Buyer has right to inspect property (typically 17 days in C.A.R. RPA)
- If buyer disapproves of conditions, buyer may request repairs or cancel
- Seller may accept, counter, or refuse repair requests
- **Active removal required** -- buyer must affirmatively remove contingency in writing

### Loan Contingency
- Buyer has specified period to obtain loan approval (typically 21 days)
- Buyer must act diligently to obtain financing
- If buyer cannot obtain financing within the period, buyer may cancel
- **Active removal required**

### Appraisal Contingency
- Property must appraise at or above purchase price (or agreed value)
- If appraisal comes in low, buyer may cancel or negotiate
- Can be waived (common in competitive markets)

### Contingency Removal Process (C.A.R.)
- Buyer must deliver written removal of contingencies within specified timeframes
- If buyer fails to remove contingencies, seller may issue a **Notice to Buyer to Perform** (NBP)
- Buyer then has 2 days to remove contingencies or cancel
- If buyer does neither, seller may cancel the agreement

---

## Counteroffers

- Seller may reject, accept, or counter any offer
- Counteroffers must be in writing (C.A.R. Form CO -- Counter Offer)
- Multiple counter offers can be issued simultaneously (C.A.R. Form SMCO)
- A counteroffer terminates the original offer
- The counteroffer must be accepted to create a binding agreement

---

## Contract Modifications

### Addenda
- Additional terms that supplement the purchase agreement
- Must be in writing and signed by all parties
- Common addenda: repair requests, extension of contingency periods, price adjustments

### Amendments
- Changes to the existing agreement after execution
- Must be in writing and signed by all parties
- C.A.R. Form AM (Amendment to Purchase Agreement)

---

## Cancellation and Remedies

### Buyer's Right to Cancel
- During contingency periods, buyer may cancel and receive deposit back
- After contingency removal, cancellation may result in forfeiture of deposit (liquidated damages -- see below)

### Seller's Right to Cancel
- If buyer fails to perform after proper notices
- If buyer fails to remove contingencies after Notice to Buyer to Perform

### Liquidated Damages
- C.A.R. RPA includes optional liquidated damages clause (requires separate initials)
- If initialed, seller's remedy for buyer's breach is limited to the deposit amount
- Maximum: 3% of purchase price for residential 1-4 units (Civil Code 1675)
- Without liquidated damages clause, seller may pursue actual damages

### Specific Performance
- Either party may seek court order compelling the other to complete the transaction
- Available only if liquidated damages clause was NOT initialed (for seller)
- Buyer may always seek specific performance

---

## Mediation and Arbitration

### Mediation (Mandatory)
- C.A.R. RPA requires mediation before any arbitration or litigation
- Party who fails to mediate before filing loses right to recover attorney fees

### Arbitration (Optional)
- Requires separate initials by both parties
- Binding -- very limited grounds for appeal
- Excludes certain claims (e.g., personal injury, eviction)

---

## Electronic Signatures

### Legal Authority
- California Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA) -- Civil Code 1633.1 et seq.
- Federal E-SIGN Act
- Electronic signatures are legally equivalent to handwritten signatures for real estate transactions
- DocuSign, Dotloop, and similar platforms are widely accepted

### Requirements
- Both parties must consent to electronic transactions
- Records must be capable of retention
- Electronic signatures must be attributable to the person who signed

## Contract Builder Implementation Notes

### Required Fields
1. Buyer name(s) and entity type
2. Seller name(s) and entity type
3. Property address and APN
4. Purchase price
5. Deposit amount and terms
6. Financing terms or cash verification
7. Closing date
8. Contingency periods (inspection, loan, appraisal)
9. Agency confirmation
10. Allocation of costs
11. Possession terms
12. Dispute resolution elections (mediation, arbitration initials)
13. Liquidated damages election (initials)

### Validation Rules
- Purchase price must be a positive number
- Deposit must not exceed 3% for liquidated damages clause
- Closing date must be in the future
- All contingency periods must be reasonable (positive integers)
- Both parties must sign (or their authorized agents with written authorization)
- Agency confirmation must be completed
- Megan's Law notice must be included
